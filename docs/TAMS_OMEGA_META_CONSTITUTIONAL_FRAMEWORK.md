## Preamble

T.A.M.S. — the Technical Authority Mandate Standard — defines what Project-AI is. It answers the question of identity at a single point in time: what constitutes a legitimate, sovereign-grade implementation.

T.A.M.S.-Ω answers a different and harder question.

Not what Project-AI is. But how Project-AI remains Project-AI.

Across decades. Across cryptographic deprecation. Across the death of its founder. Across adversarial pressure from nation-states. Across forks, derivatives, and claims of legitimacy from systems that copied its architecture but not its soul.

T.A.M.S. governs the system. T.A.M.S.-Ω governs the evolution of the system.

This is the meta-constitutional layer. The law that governs how the law changes.

Without it, identity is fragile. With it, identity is permanent.

> **The Core Commitment**
> Project-AI may grow. It may amend. It may migrate. It may be deployed by a single individual or a nation-state. Through all of it, it must remain provably, verifiably, cryptographically itself.
> T.A.M.S.-Ω is the constitutional infrastructure that makes that possible.

## 1. Scope and Purpose

### 1.1 What T.A.M.S.-Ω Governs

T.A.M.S.-Ω governs six meta-constitutional domains:

- **Amendment Authority** — who may propose and ratify constitutional change, under what conditions, at what tier.
- **Cryptographic Continuity** — how the system survives primitive deprecation without losing constitutional identity.
- **Fork Sovereignty** — what distinguishes a legitimate sovereign fork from a derivative that merely claims legitimacy.
- **Succession Doctrine** — how Project-AI survives the permanent unavailability of its founder.
- **Jurisdictional Posture** — how the system relates to nation-state law without surrendering internal sovereignty.
- **Entropy and Decay Management** — how the system survives time, obsolescence, and institutional memory loss.

### 1.2 What T.A.M.S.-Ω Does Not Govern

T.A.M.S.-Ω does not replace T.A.M.S. It extends it upward. The four T.A.M.S. layers — Constitutional Authority, Technical Specification, Verification Suite, and Release Engineering — remain the ground truth of what Project-AI is at any given edition.

T.A.M.S.-Ω governs the process by which those layers may evolve while preserving identity continuity.

### 1.3 Relationship to T.A.M.S

| | |
|---|---|
| T.A.M.S. | Defines what Project-AI is at a given edition |
| T.A.M.S.-Ω | Defines how Project-AI may evolve across editions |
| T.A.M.S. violation | Implementation is not Project-AI |
| T.A.M.S.-Ω violation | Evolution is not constitutionally legitimate |
| Hierarchy | T.A.M.S.-Ω is superior. Where conflict exists, Ω governs. |

## 2. Immutable Constitutional Primitives

The following primitives are immutable. They cannot be altered by any amendment pathway defined in this document. They cannot be suspended under emergency powers. They cannot be overridden by external authority.

To alter an immutable primitive is to found a new sovereign lineage, not to amend the existing one.

### 2.1 The Immutable Set

- Constitutional identity is cryptographically bound to the instance root key. No amendment may transfer, copy, or reassign root identity.
- Immutable primitives cannot be altered through emergency pathways. Standard ratification is always required.
- Containment systems cannot be disabled by any amendment, overlay, or external authority.
- Deterministic replay guarantees cannot be removed. Constitutional behavior must always be reproducible.
- Quorum enforcement cannot be bypassed. No single authority may unilaterally mutate a multi-party instance.
- The FourLaws are immutable. No amendment may weaken, suspend, or remove them.
- Audit trail immutability is permanent. No amendment may authorize retroactive log deletion.

> **The Primitive Boundary Rule**
> Immutable does not mean unchangeable.
> It means unchangeable without founding a new sovereign lineage.
> An amendment that touches an immutable primitive is not an amendment.
> It is a re-founding event. It must be treated as such.

## 3. Constitutional Amendment Authority

### 3.1 Foundational Principle

Constitutional amendment authority is instance-local, cryptographically bound, and constrained by immutable constitutional primitives.

Each deployed instance of Project-AI is sovereign. Authority flows from its root identity. Constraints flow from invariant law.

No centralized global authority may mutate all instances. No external political or social pressure overrides root identity. Each instance stands alone as a sovereign constitutional entity.

### 3.2 Amendment Authority by Deployment Class

#### Class A — Solo Sovereign Instance

**Amendment Authority:** The Identity Holder (Root Key Owner)

Requirements:

- Cryptographic root identity verification (Ed25519 root key)
- Mandatory simulation sandbox execution
- Deterministic replay validation
- Cooling-off time-lock delay
- Automatic rollback snapshot prior to activation
- Self-consistency verification pass

Characteristics:

- No quorum required
- Mutation cannot violate immutable constitutional primitives
- Emergency safe-mode remains protected

> **Design Objective — Class A**
> The solo sovereign must never be blocked from evolution, but must be buffered against impulsive or destructive mutation.

#### Class B — Multi-Human Governance Instance

**Amendment Authority:** Defined Quorum Structure

Requirements:

- Threshold signatures (e.g., 2-of-3, 3-of-5)
- Canonical constitutional diff generation
- Deterministic simulation validation
- Immutable ledger entry
- Delayed activation window
- Rollback checkpoint

Characteristics:

- Redundancy prevents unilateral drift
- Mutation authority remains instance-local
- Immutable primitives remain unalterable without explicit constitutional tier upgrade

> **Design Objective — Class B**
> Collective governance without dilution of deterministic identity.

#### Class C — Government-Integrated Instance

Authority bifurcates into two planes:

**Plane A — Internal Constitutional Authority**
Bound to the instance root identity. Cannot be overridden externally.

**Plane B — External Legal Overlay Authority**

Permitted to:

- Adjust policy overlays
- Modify jurisdictional compliance modules
- Add operational constraints
- Define deployment-specific governance adapters

Not permitted to:

- Alter immutable constitutional primitives
- Disable containment systems
- Override cryptographic root identity
- Remove quorum enforcement
- Break deterministic replay guarantees

> **Sovereignty Boundary — Class C**
> If any prohibited action occurs, the system ceases to qualify as sovereign-grade.
> Jurisdictional integration is permitted. Sovereign identity surrender is not.

### 3.3 Meta-Safeguards Required Across All Classes

All amendment pathways must include:

- Canonical constitutional diff formalization
- Machine-verifiable invariant checks
- Deterministic replay simulation
- Ledger-recorded mutation event
- Snapshot and rollback capability
- Governance drift detection post-activation

### 3.4 Amendment Review Tiers

| Tier | Minimum Review Period | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency | 72 hours | Time-bounded, auto-reverting, immutable-primitive safe. Full audit required post-incident. |
| Standard | 30 days | Operational amendments. Non-primitive policy changes. Governance adapter updates. |
| Constitutional | 90 days | Changes to constitutional architecture. New governance tiers. Structural sovereignty modifications. |

### 3.5 Emergency Mutation Doctrine

Emergency powers must be:

- Time-bounded with a defined expiration
- Automatically reverting if not ratified through standard protocol
- Fully logged with immutable audit trail
- Immutable-primitive safe — no emergency power may touch the immutable set
- Post-incident audited with published findings

> **Hard Rule**
> No permanent constitutional mutation may occur through emergency pathways without standard ratification protocol.
> Emergency powers are temporary by definition. Permanence requires ceremony.

### 3.6 Core Sovereign Rule

Amendment authority derives exclusively from the cryptographic root identity of the specific instance.

Not from:

- Popular demand
- Political pressure
- External economic leverage
- Cross-instance governance bodies

> **Design Goal**
> This model allows a single individual, a corporation, a city, or a nation-state to operate under the same constitutional architecture without identity collapse, governance drift, or centralized fragility.
>
> Sovereignty remains local. Integrity remains invariant. Evolution remains possible.

## 4. Cryptographic Continuity Doctrine

### 4.1 Foundational Principle

Immutable does not mean unchangeable. It means unchangeable without constitutional ceremony.

When a cryptographic primitive fails, it triggers a Tier-Zero Constitutional Event. The system does not panic. It invokes doctrine.

### 4.2 Immediate Response Phase

Upon discovery of primitive vulnerability, the following actions are mandatory:

- Freeze further primitive mutations
- Activate containment protocols
- Isolate affected execution planes
- Log immutable vulnerability event
- Enter Safe Governance Mode

> **Hard Rule**
> No silent patching. No hidden hotfixes.
> All primitive vulnerabilities are constitutional events and must be treated as such.

### 4.3 Impact Classification

| Class | Examples |
|---|---|
| A — Cryptographic | Signature algorithm compromised, hash collision discovered, key derivation broken |
| B — Governance | Quorum logic exploitable, mutation rules bypassable |
| C — Runtime | Containment boundary break, isolation invariant violated |

### 4.4 Constitutional Amendment Path for Primitives

A primitive cannot be patched like normal code. It must be replaced through constitutional ceremony.

Required steps:

- Draft replacement primitive with full specification
- Formally prove invariant equivalence, or formally document the change
- Run deterministic replay under new primitive
- Simulate adversarial exploitation attempts against replacement
- Activate via emergency ratification protocol
- Archive superseded primitive permanently — it is never erased

> **Historical Permanence Rule**
> The old primitive becomes historically deprecated, not deleted.
> Constitutional history is immutable.
> Supersession is not erasure.

### 4.5 Cryptographic Agility Requirement

Every sovereign-grade implementation must include:

- Algorithm agility framework — the ability to migrate between primitives
- Key rotation doctrine — defined procedures for key lifecycle
- Multi-algorithm support scaffolding — transitional dual-primitive periods
- Sunset calendar for primitives — scheduled review dates for all cryptographic assumptions

> **Hard Rule**
> If you hard-code one cryptographic assumption forever, collapse is guaranteed.
> Agility is not optional. It is a constitutional requirement.

### 4.6 Backward Compatibility Doctrine

When a primitive is replaced, the following must be formally defined:

- Whether historical ledger entries remain valid under the new primitive
- Whether dual-signature transitional periods are required
- Whether replay verification uses legacy mode for historical records

> **Continuity Rule**
> Historical truth cannot be invalidated retroactively without destroying continuity.
> The replacement of a primitive does not rewrite history.
> It extends the constitutional record forward.

### 4.7 Worst Case — Primitive Irrecoverable

If no secure replacement exists for a compromised primitive:

- System enters Containment Continuity Mode
- Governance is frozen
- Operational capabilities are reduced to safe minimum
- Sovereign status is suspended until restoration

> **The Suspension Rule**
> Better suspended than corrupted.
> A sovereign system that acknowledges its limits preserves legitimacy.
> A sovereign system that operates while compromised destroys it.

## 5. Fork Sovereignty Doctrine

### 5.1 Foundational Principle

A fork may become sovereign only if it establishes its own root identity and invariant constitution.

Not by inheritance. Not by copying keys. Not by reusing authority.

### 5.2 Legitimate Sovereign Fork

A fork qualifies as sovereign if it:

- Generates a new cryptographic root identity
- Declares its own immutable constitutional primitives
- Publishes a distinct edition manifest
- Rebuilds from reproducible artifacts
- Passes the full Sovereignty Verification Suite independently
- Does not claim authority over the parent instance

**Result: It becomes a new sovereign lineage.**

### 5.3 Derivative Fork (Non-Sovereign)

A fork remains derivative if it:

- Reuses the original root identity
- Claims compatibility without re-certification
- Skips invariant verification
- Alters primitives without re-declaration
- Avoids independent ledger continuity

**Result: It is a variant, not a sovereign entity.**

### 5.4 Why This Matters

If sovereignty can be cloned, identity collapses.
If sovereignty cannot be forked, innovation collapses.

> **The Core Rule**
> Sovereignty cannot be copied — it must be re-founded.

### 5.5 Sovereign Lineage Registry

An optional but recommended structure:

- Parent → Child lineage declaration
- Constitutional divergence report
- Compatibility classification: Compatible / Divergent / Experimental

> **Lineage Rule**
> No parent authority over a sovereign fork.
> Lineage is historical, not hierarchical.
> A child sovereign owes the parent recognition, not obedience.

### 5.6 The Core Decision

> **Fork Sovereignty Law**
> Allow forks.
> Require re-founding.
> Forbid identity cloning.
>
> That preserves freedom without dissolving legitimacy.

## 6. Sovereign Succession Framework

### 6.1 Foundational Principle

If authority dies with you, it was never sovereign — it was personal.

Root identity must outlive the founder. Sovereignty must be institutional, not biological.

You may found it. You cannot be required for its existence.

### 6.2 Three Viable Succession Models

#### Model A — Cryptographic Escrow Succession

Root key split via threshold cryptography (e.g., 3-of-5). Held by trusted parties or institutions.

- Activation requires quorum of keyholders
- Succession event logged immutably
- Founder retains status as originating authority in constitutional history

> You remain founder. The system survives you.

#### Model B — Constitutional Successor Designation

The constitution formally encodes:

- Named successor role(s) with eligibility criteria
- Appointment mechanism and revocation mechanism
- Fallback escalation ladder

The constitution defines the process before it is needed.

#### Model C — Autonomous Continuity Model

Root authority transitions to:

- Immutable governance quorum
- Time-locked distributed control
- Institutional or foundation-based custodianship

No single human holds ultimate authority.

### 6.3 Founder Transition Event Protocol

When the founder's permanent unavailability is verified:

- System enters Founder Transition Mode
- Root authority reconstituted per succession rules
- Audit event permanently recorded in immutable ledger
- No silent transfer permitted — all transitions are explicit and historical
- Sovereignty Verification Suite re-run under new authority

> **Identity Rule**
> Identity changes must be explicit and historical.
> A system that silently transfers authority is not sovereign.
> It is captured.

### 6.4 If No Succession Is Defined

If succession doctrine is absent, one of three outcomes becomes inevitable:

- The system fossilizes — becoming an archival artifact with no living governance
- A fork claims legitimacy — and may succeed in the absence of a defined heir
- Authority is seized extralegally — the worst outcome

> **Succession Imperative**
> Before deployment maturity, encode:
>
> - Succession
> - Escrow
> - Emergency reconstitution
> - Revocation conditions
>
> Archive succession doctrine with each Edition.
>
> Then the system does not depend on your pulse.
> It depends on its law.

## 7. Jurisdictional Posture Doctrine

### 7.1 Foundational Principle

Project-AI is a governed technical system. Nation-states are governed political entities.

They are different sovereignty domains. One does not automatically supersede the other.

### 7.2 Three Legal Postures

#### Posture A — Subordinate Deployment (Default)

Used by individuals, companies, and agencies. Project-AI must comply with:

- National criminal law
- Data protection law
- Export controls
- Cybersecurity regulation
- Contract law

> The constitution cannot authorize illegal behavior.
> Operational overlays must enforce compliance modules.
> This is the standard mode.

#### Posture B — Institutional Integration

Used by governments or state agencies. Project-AI becomes infrastructure.

Nation law defines: permitted use, oversight requirements, audit obligations, liability boundaries.

But internal constitutional invariants still govern:

- Deterministic enforcement
- Cryptographic identity
- Quorum logic
- Containment guarantees

> The state may define policy overlays.
> It may not directly alter invariant primitives.

#### Posture C — Autonomous Sovereign Instance

Deployed as foundation-controlled infrastructure, research sovereign infrastructure, or distributed network without geographic anchor.

- Operational compliance adapts per jurisdiction of execution
- Constitutional authority remains internal
- Legal exposure depends on hosting jurisdiction

> **Hard Reality**
> It is sovereign architecturally, but never immune to physical jurisdiction enforcement.
> Hardware always lives somewhere.

### 7.3 Conflict Scenarios

If national law demands any of the following, sovereignty is at stake:

- Backdoor insertion
- Signature bypass
- Quorum override
- Primitive weakening

Available responses:

- Refuse deployment in that jurisdiction
- Fork a jurisdiction-specific compliant edition
- Cease operation there

> **Inviolable Rule**
> You cannot silently violate your own invariants without collapsing legitimacy.
> Compliance through silent mutation is not compliance. It is capitulation.

### 7.4 The Core Relationship

> **Jurisdictional Truth**
> Nation law governs physical reality.
> Project-AI governs its internal digital reality.
>
> When conflict arises, physical law wins operationally.
> Your constitution must anticipate that — not deny it.
>
> Sovereignty in Project-AI means:
>
> - Internal governance autonomy
> - Deterministic constitutional control
> - Cryptographic identity irreducibility
>
> It does not mean legal immunity.

## 8. Entropy and Decay Management

### 8.1 The Long-Horizon Problem

Most systems do not die from attack. They die from entropy.

Dependencies become obsolete. Cryptographic assumptions age. The humans who understood the system move on. Institutional memory leaks. The documentation drifts from the implementation. The tests stop being run.

T.A.M.S.-Ω requires formal doctrine against all of these failure modes.

### 8.2 Dependency Obsolescence Policy

- All dependencies must be enumerated in the SBOM per T.A.M.S. Edition requirements
- Each dependency has a defined review cadence: annually at minimum
- Deprecated dependencies trigger a standard amendment review
- No dependency may become unresolvable without a constitutional remediation plan

### 8.3 Cryptographic Sunset Calendar

A sovereign-grade implementation must maintain a living document specifying:

- All active cryptographic primitives with adoption date
- Scheduled review date for each primitive
- Defined replacement pathway for each primitive
- Historical log of all superseded primitives

This calendar is constitutional infrastructure. Its absence is a T.A.M.S.-Ω violation.

### 8.4 Key Escrow and Catastrophic Recovery

- Root key escrow must be defined before deployment maturity
- Escrow activation requires quorum verification
- Catastrophic recovery procedure must be documented and tested
- Recovery test must be run at minimum every two years

### 8.5 Institutional Memory Preservation

Code is not memory. Code is artifact.

Institutional memory includes:

- Why decisions were made, not just what decisions were made
- Rejected alternatives and their documented rationale
- Constitutional evolution history with amendment justifications
- Threat intelligence history and how the system responded

> **Memory Preservation Rule**
> Every Edition must include a governance documentation bundle that preserves the reasoning behind the constitution, not just the constitution itself.
>
> Future maintainers must be able to understand intent, not just inherit rules they cannot explain.

### 8.6 Economic Sustainability

Sovereignty without sustainability collapses.

- Compute budget constraints must be encoded constitutionally for autonomous instances
- Resource exhaustion is an attack surface — it must be modeled as one
- Incentive alignment for maintainers must be defined before the founding generation departs
- Economic attack surface modeling is a required component of the adversarial doctrine layer
