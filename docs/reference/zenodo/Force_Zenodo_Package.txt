DESCRIPTION (paste into the Description field):
────────────────────────────────────────────────
The prevailing discourse surrounding AI safety assumes that harm can be mitigated
through restriction or avoidance of dangerous concepts. This paper challenges that
assumption as structurally insufficient and proposes a governance-first model in
which AGI systems possess full situational awareness while being bound by structural
constraints—not ethical guidelines—governing when and how force may be applied.

The paper makes four contributions: (1) a critique of instruction-based and
value-alignment approaches as insufficient for force governance; (2) a graduated
response architecture with cryptographically-enforced authority tokens; (3) an
explicit four-layer authority chain separating token issuance from system execution;
and (4) an honest treatment of implementation limits including enforcement layer
purity, adversarial robustness, latency tradeoffs, emergent capability indirection,
and multi-agent coordination at deployment scale.

The graduated response hierarchy maps directly to LOAC principles of necessity,
proportionality, and distinction. Authority tokens are cryptographically signed,
time-bounded, and continuously validated against a live revocation endpoint.
The enforcement layer is architecturally distinct from the reasoning layer, with
honest acknowledgment that perfect separation is aspirational.

Six open problems are identified: enforcement layer purity, sub-second decision
latency, multi-agent token coordination, adversarial token forgery, emergent
capability indirection, and institutional capture of the sovereign authority layer.

This paper is part of the Project-AI constitutional governance corpus (Papers 1–14,
Zenodo Thirstys Projects Community).
