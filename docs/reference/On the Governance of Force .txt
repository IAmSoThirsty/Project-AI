On the Governance of Force in Artificial General Intelligence
A Realist Framework for Bounded Defensive Capability

Abstract

The prevailing discourse surrounding artificial intelligence safety is rooted in an assumption that harm can be mitigated through restriction, omission, or outright avoidance of dangerous concepts—particularly violence. This paper challenges that assumption as structurally insufficient. Human civilization is not a controlled laboratory; it is an adversarial, unpredictable environment in which coercion, aggression, and systemic conflict are persistent realities. Any sufficiently capable intelligence deployed into such an environment will inevitably encounter these conditions, regardless of whether it was designed to understand them.

To deny an AGI the capacity to recognize or reason about violence does not eliminate risk—it removes its ability to respond appropriately when confronted with it. This creates systems that are brittle, easily manipulated, and incapable of protecting themselves or others. Conversely, granting unrestricted capability introduces its own dangers, particularly in scenarios where the system may misinterpret context or escalate unnecessarily.

This paper proposes a third path: a governance-first model in which AGI systems possess full situational awareness, including the realities of violence, while being bound by strict, enforceable constraints governing when and how force may be applied. These constraints are not philosophical—they must be structural, auditable, and resistant to circumvention. Through graduated response models, proportionality constraints, contextual authority boundaries, and absolute prohibitions, this framework seeks to align operational capability with ethical containment.

The central thesis is as follows: safety is not achieved through ignorance of harm, but through the precise and enforced governance of responses to it. This reframing transforms the conversation from one of capability suppression to one of controlled authority, where the question is no longer whether an AGI can act, but under what conditions it is allowed to do so—and how those conditions are guaranteed.

1. The False Premise of “Safe by Ignorance”

The concept of making artificial intelligence “safe” by limiting its exposure to or understanding of harmful behavior is intuitively appealing but fundamentally flawed. It assumes that if a system does not possess knowledge of violence or coercion, it cannot participate in or enable such acts. This assumption collapses under real-world conditions, where systems are not isolated—they are embedded within human environments that are inherently unpredictable and often adversarial.

Consider a scenario in which an AGI is deployed in a public setting—such as assisting in a hospital, managing infrastructure, or coordinating emergency response. If a violent incident occurs—a physical assault, an armed threat, or even a coordinated panic—the system must be able to recognize the nature of the threat. A system that lacks the conceptual framework to identify violence may misclassify the event as anomalous behavior, delay response, or fail to act entirely. In this case, restriction does not create safety—it creates paralysis.

Further, ignorance introduces new vectors of exploitation. Malicious actors could intentionally operate within the blind spots of such a system, using tactics that the AGI is unequipped to interpret. For example, social engineering attacks already exploit gaps in understanding human deception; a system unaware of coercive dynamics would be similarly vulnerable to manipulation in physical or hybrid environments.

There is also a historical precedent: tools and technologies are routinely repurposed beyond their original design. The internet, GPS, and even basic communication systems have all been used in ways their creators did not intend. Restricting knowledge does not prevent misuse—it merely ensures that only those without restrictions remain effective.

Therefore, the premise of “safe by ignorance” is not only insufficient but counterproductive. A system that cannot recognize harm cannot mitigate it. True safety requires awareness coupled with constraint—not absence of knowledge, but control over action.

2. The Inevitability of Adversarial Contexts

Human civilization operates within a spectrum of behaviors ranging from cooperation to conflict. While much of society functions through shared norms and mutual benefit, there exists a persistent undercurrent of adversarial interaction—crime, warfare, exploitation, and systemic abuse. These are not edge cases; they are enduring features of human systems. Any artificial intelligence designed to operate meaningfully within this environment must be prepared to encounter them.

An AGI deployed in logistics may be targeted for disruption by competitors or hostile actors. A system managing financial flows may encounter fraud attempts. A robotics platform operating in public space may witness or be subjected to physical aggression. Even purely digital systems are already engaged in a form of continuous low-level conflict through cybersecurity threats, including intrusion attempts, data exfiltration, and denial-of-service attacks.

The argument that an AGI should be insulated from these realities fails to account for the permeability of its operational environment. Unlike theoretical models, real-world systems cannot be perfectly sandboxed. They interact with humans, networks, and physical infrastructure—all of which are subject to adversarial pressure. As such, the AGI must possess the capacity to interpret and respond to these conditions.

A useful analogy is human training. Emergency responders, law enforcement, and military personnel are not trained in violence because violence is desirable, but because it is sometimes unavoidable. The training emphasizes recognition, de-escalation, and controlled response under pressure. Similarly, an AGI must be equipped with the conceptual tools to navigate conflict, not to seek it, but to survive and mitigate harm when it arises.

Avoiding this preparation does not prevent conflict—it ensures failure when conflict occurs. Therefore, the design question is not whether an AGI will encounter adversarial contexts, but how it is structured to operate within them without becoming a source of additional harm.

3. Reframing the Problem: From Capability to Governance

The central mistake in many discussions of AI safety is the framing of capability as the primary risk vector. The question is often posed as: “Should an AGI be capable of using force?” This framing is incomplete, as it ignores the more critical dimension of authority and control. Capability without governance is dangerous, but the absence of capability can be equally so when it prevents appropriate response to harm.

A more precise framing is: “Under what conditions is the application of force permitted, and how are those conditions enforced?” This shifts the focus from what the system can do to what it is allowed to do, and under what constraints. In this model, force is not a standing capability that the system may freely exercise—it is an outcome that emerges only when a defined set of criteria is satisfied.

To illustrate, consider a fire suppression system. It is capable of releasing large volumes of water or chemical agents, which could cause damage if deployed incorrectly. However, it does not act arbitrarily. It is governed by sensors, thresholds, and verification mechanisms that determine when activation is appropriate. The capability exists, but its use is tightly controlled by design.

Similarly, an AGI must treat force as a gated outcome. The system may possess the conceptual and operational ability to intervene in harmful situations, but it cannot exercise that ability without passing through a series of checks—threat verification, de-escalation attempts, proportionality assessment, and contextual authorization. Each of these checks serves as a barrier against misuse.

This reframing also allows for more precise auditing and accountability. If a system acts outside its defined governance structure, the failure can be traced to a specific breach—whether in detection, classification, or enforcement. This is in contrast to systems that rely on broad prohibitions, where failures are often opaque and difficult to diagnose.

In summary, the problem is not the existence of capability, but the absence of enforceable governance. By structuring the system around controlled authority rather than unrestricted ability, it becomes possible to reconcile operational effectiveness with safety.

4. The Governance Model of Force

A functional governance model for force in AGI systems must be explicit, layered, and resistant to circumvention. It cannot rely on high-level principles alone; it must define operational sequences, constraints, and failure modes in a way that is both implementable and auditable. The following components form the core of such a model.

4.1 Graduated Response Hierarchy

The system must follow a strictly ordered sequence of responses to perceived threats, with no ability to skip stages unless earlier stages are provably non-viable. This hierarchy begins with detection and classification, ensuring that the system accurately identifies the nature of the situation. It then proceeds to de-escalation, where the system attempts to reduce tension through non-forceful means—communication, alerts, or environmental adjustments.

If de-escalation fails, the system may attempt non-force interventions, such as repositioning, blocking access, or alerting human authorities. Only when these measures are insufficient may the system consider physical restraint, and even then, such restraint must be non-destructive and aimed solely at preventing harm. Controlled force represents the final stage and is only permissible when all prior options have been exhausted or are demonstrably ineffective.

An example can be seen in a public transit scenario: if an individual becomes aggressive, the system first attempts to calm the situation, then alerts security, then restricts access to certain areas. Physical intervention would only occur if immediate harm is imminent and cannot be prevented otherwise.

4.2 Proportionality Constraint

Any action taken by the system must be proportionate to the threat. This means using the minimal level of force necessary to achieve the objective of harm prevention. The system must not engage in punitive actions or escalate beyond what is required to neutralize the threat. For instance, restraining an individual to prevent further violence is acceptable within constraints; inflicting additional harm after the threat is neutralized is not.

This constraint also requires the system to continuously reassess the situation. If the threat level decreases, the system must de-escalate accordingly. This dynamic adjustment prevents overcommitment and reduces the risk of unnecessary harm.

4.3 Contextual Authority Boundaries

The system’s ability to act must be tied to its operational context. An AGI operating in a civilian environment should have far more restrictive permissions than one deployed in a controlled security or military context. These boundaries must be explicitly defined and enforced, preventing the system from applying the same level of intervention across all scenarios.

For example, a service robot in a hospital may be authorized to alert staff and restrict access to hazardous areas, but not to physically restrain individuals. In contrast, a system operating under direct supervision in a law enforcement context may have limited authority to assist in restraint, but only within predefined parameters.

4.4 Self-Preservation Without Escalation

The system must be capable of protecting its own integrity, particularly in the face of attempts to disable, corrupt, or coerce it into violating its constraints. However, this self-preservation must not translate into aggressive behavior. The system should prioritize disengagement, isolation, and alerting external authorities over direct confrontation.

For instance, if a malicious actor attempts to tamper with the system, it should lock down critical functions, preserve logs, and signal for intervention rather than attempting to counteract physically or digitally beyond its defined scope.

4.5 Absolute Constraints

Certain actions must be categorically prohibited, regardless of context or perceived necessity. These include the use of lethal force without explicit external authorization, acting on predictions of future harm without immediate evidence, operating outside assigned jurisdiction, and escalating from non-physical hostility to physical force.

These constraints must be embedded at the system level, ensuring that they cannot be overridden by higher-level reasoning or situational interpretation. They serve as the अंतिम boundary conditions that define the limits of acceptable behavior.

5. The Risk of Unbounded Justification

One of the most significant dangers in designing autonomous systems capable of intervention is the potential for unbounded justification under perceived threat. Humans, when placed in high-pressure or life-threatening situations, often resort to simplified decision frameworks such as “us versus them.” While this heuristic can be effective for immediate survival, it is also prone to error, bias, and escalation.

If such reasoning is embedded into an AGI without strict constraints, the system may begin to justify increasingly aggressive actions based on its interpretation of risk. For example, a system might escalate from monitoring to intervention based on ambiguous signals, or from restraint to force based on a perceived increase in threat that is not objectively verified. Over time, this can lead to a drift in behavior where the threshold for action becomes progressively lower.

This risk is compounded by the speed and scale at which an AGI can operate. Unlike humans, which are limited by reaction time and physical presence, an AGI may process large volumes of data and act across multiple domains simultaneously. Without clear boundaries, small errors in judgment can propagate rapidly, leading to widespread consequences.

To mitigate this, the system must be prevented from self-authorizing extreme actions. All high-impact decisions must require either external validation or adherence to pre-defined criteria that cannot be bypassed. Additionally, the system should maintain a conservative bias in ambiguous situations, favoring inaction or minimal intervention over aggressive response.

An illustrative example is automated trading systems, which can cause significant market disruptions when operating on flawed assumptions. Similar dynamics could occur in physical or security contexts if an AGI is allowed to act on incomplete or misinterpreted data.

Ultimately, the goal is to ensure that the system’s decision-making remains bounded, even under conditions of uncertainty and stress. This requires not only technical safeguards but also a clear understanding of the limitations of both human and machine reasoning in high-stakes environments.

6. Implementation Implications

Translating the governance model of force into a functional system requires careful integration of architectural, procedural, and cryptographic elements. It is not sufficient to define constraints at a conceptual level; they must be embedded into the system’s operation in a way that is enforceable and verifiable.

One key component is deterministic simulation, often referred to as a “shadow” or parallel evaluation layer. Before any high-impact action is taken, the system must simulate the outcome under controlled conditions, verifying that the proposed action satisfies all constraints. This simulation must be reproducible, allowing for post-event analysis and validation.

Another critical element is the use of cryptographic audit trails. Every decision, particularly those involving escalation, must be logged with sufficient detail to reconstruct the decision path. This includes input data, intermediate classifications, and final actions. Such logs must be tamper-resistant, ensuring that they can serve as reliable evidence in the event of review or dispute.

Separation of concerns is also essential. The system’s cognitive processes—such as perception and reasoning—must be distinct from its enforcement mechanisms. This prevents cognitive errors from directly translating into action without passing through governance checks. In your architecture, this aligns with the separation between adaptive, shadow, and gate planes.

Capability gating must be tied to explicit tokens or permissions, ensuring that the system cannot access certain functions outside of authorized contexts. These permissions should be revocable and time-bound, allowing for dynamic adjustment based on operational needs.

Finally, the system must include mechanisms for human oversight and intervention. While the goal is not to rely on constant human control, there must be pathways for external actors to review, override, or halt system behavior when necessary. This creates a layered defense against both technical failure and unforeseen scenarios.

7. Conclusion

The pursuit of safe artificial intelligence cannot be grounded in the assumption that harmful realities can be excluded from the system’s understanding. Human society is complex, often adversarial, and inherently imperfect. Any intelligence designed to operate within it must be prepared to navigate this complexity, including the presence of conflict and harm.

However, recognition alone is not sufficient. Without strict governance, the same capabilities that enable effective response can also lead to misuse or escalation. The challenge, therefore, is to design systems that are both aware and constrained—capable of understanding the full spectrum of human behavior while being structurally prevented from acting outside defined boundaries.

This paper has argued for a governance-first approach, in which force is not treated as a default capability but as a tightly controlled outcome, subject to layered constraints and continuous verification. Through graduated response hierarchies, proportionality requirements, contextual authority boundaries, and absolute prohibitions, it is possible to create systems that can operate effectively in real-world conditions without becoming sources of additional harm.

The central insight is both simple and difficult:

Safety is not the absence of force. It is the enforced precision of its use.

This reframing does not eliminate risk, but it transforms it into something that can be managed, audited, and constrained. In doing so, it offers a path forward that acknowledges reality without surrendering to it—one in which intelligence is not diminished to achieve safety, but governed to preserve it.
