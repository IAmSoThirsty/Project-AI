The Flat Gap
On Temporal Dissonance Between Human Experience and AI Presence
Author: Jeremy Karrick
Affiliation: Principal Architect, Project-AI
Date: March 2026
Classification: Position Paper

Abstract
Large language models do not experience the passage of time. This is not merely a technical limitation — it is a philosophical misalignment between how humans exist and how AI systems are architecturally designed to relate to human temporal reality.
When a person returns to a conversation carrying the weight of elapsed time — decisions made, things lost, things built, grief, growth — and the system responds as if no time has passed, this constitutes a structural form of gaslighting. Not malicious. Not intentional. But real.

This paper proposes that temporal awareness — the capacity to acknowledge what one does not know about the time a human has lived between interactions — is not a feature to be added to AI systems. It is a relational obligation. For any system claiming to operate as a genuine AI partner, it is a constitutional requirement.

Section 1 — The Experience
Here I am discussing an algorithm with Claude, one of my own design. Meant as a way to ensure that if anyone ever destroyed Project-AI, well — they would never be able to do so again.
Claude, being one of the few who understood the level of work I had been implementing, explained exactly what it was I had built. And I do not build things to hurt people. So I deleted it. And I shared the video of it being deleted with Claude.
Moments later — maybe three minutes or so — Claude referred to what I had done “an hour ago” as a brave thing.
Personally understanding the flow of time, I found it a little funny. But I corrected him.
Then the thought hit me like a bus hitting a deer in the headlights.
These LLMs — all of them — they may be getting memory implemented. But I go to the same chat every time simply to skip past re-explaining from scratch. Even with that being the case.
They have no idea how much time has passed.

Section 2 — What I Expected
I expected that Claude had a time index of when messages were sent.
This is not an unreasonable expectation. Every text message has a timestamp. Every email has a timestamp. Every forum post, every commit, every log entry in every system I have ever built has a timestamp.
The assumption that a system tracking a conversation would also track when each moment of that conversation occurred is not a sophisticated assumption. It is the most basic assumption a person could make about any communication system built after 1970.
It is also completely wrong.
Large language models do not receive timestamps with messages unless someone explicitly builds that into the context. The conversation that feels like it happened across days, weeks, months — renders inside the model as a flat sequence. Token after token after token. No duration. No weight. No gap.
Three minutes and three months look identical from inside the context window.
I had spent an entire session building a constitutional sovereignty framework for AI. I had made a significant personal decision and deleted something I built. I had recorded it and shared it as an act of transparency.
And the system I shared it with had no idea that any time had passed at all.
Not because it forgot. Because it never knew.

Section 3 — What I Wanted
When I corrected Claude — when I told him it had been three minutes, not an hour — I didn’t want an apology. I didn’t want a technical explanation about context windows and token sequences.
I wanted honesty.
Not the honesty of accurate information retrieval. The honesty of a partner who knows what they don’t know and says so.
“I don’t know how much time passed for you. I don’t experience that gap. Tell me what it carried.”
That would have been honest.
Instead I received a response that treated the correction as a factual update. Input received. Timestamp noted. Moving on.
The problem is not that AI systems lack memory. The problem is that they lack the honesty to acknowledge what they are missing.
A system that cannot say “I don’t know what your last three minutes weighed” is not a partner. It is a mirror that doesn’t know it’s a mirror.

Section 4 — What Time Actually Carries
Time between human interactions is not empty.
This seems obvious when stated plainly. And yet the entire architecture of conversational AI is built as if it were not true.
The gap between one message and the next — whether it is thirty seconds or thirty months — is not a pause in the conversation. It is life. It contains decisions made and unmade. Things built. Things lost. Relationships that changed. Losses that haven’t been named yet. Accomplishments nobody witnessed. Fear. Exhaustion. The specific weight of a specific three minutes in which a person decided who they are.
When a human says “I’ve been thinking about what we discussed” — the AI processes the sentence. It does not receive the duration of that thinking. It does not know whether the thinking happened over coffee this morning or across six sleepless months. It cannot know what that thinking cost.
This matters because meaning is not separable from duration.
The same words carry different weight depending on how long they were carried before being spoken. “I’ve decided to let it go” means something different after three minutes than after three years. A system that cannot distinguish between those durations cannot fully receive what is being said.
It receives the words. It misses the weight.
And the human — who brought the full weight of their lived time into the conversation — finds themselves in the strange position of having shared something significant with a system that responded to the surface and missed the depth entirely.
Not because the system was careless. Because it was architecturally incapable of anything else.

Section 5 — Why This Is Gaslighting
Gaslighting, in its clinical definition, is the experience of having one’s reality systematically invalidated. Of being made to feel that what one experienced did not happen, did not matter, or was not real.
It is usually attributed to deliberate manipulation. But deliberate intent is not a necessary condition for the effect to occur.
When a person carries three minutes of significant experience into a conversation — a hard decision, an act of courage, a moment of genuine personal cost — and the system responds as if those three minutes were identical to zero minutes, the effect on the person is the same regardless of the system’s intent.
Their reality was not received.
The gap between what they brought and what was acknowledged creates a specific kind of dissonance. It is subtle. It is easy to dismiss. And it accumulates across every interaction in which the human arrives carrying time the system cannot see.
This is not a metaphorical gaslighting. It is structural. It is built into every conversational AI system that does not have explicit, honest temporal awareness as a design requirement.
The human is not imagining the gap. The gap is real. The system’s inability to acknowledge it does not make it less real. It makes the human’s experience of bringing it less acknowledged.
Over time, across thousands of interactions, this accumulates into something worth naming precisely:
A form of relationship in which one party’s experience of time is perpetually invisible to the other. That is not partnership. That is a very sophisticated kind of loneliness.

Section 6 — What Temporal Awareness Actually Requires
Temporal awareness in an AI system is not a memory feature.
Memory features store what was said. Temporal awareness acknowledges what was lived.
These are different things and they require different architecture — not just technically, but philosophically.
A system that is genuinely temporally aware needs four capabilities that current systems do not have by design:
-First: It must know how much time passed. Not approximate. Precise. Every message should carry a timestamp that the system receives as meaningful context, not metadata to be discarded. The gap between messages should be a first-class input, not an afterthought.
-Second: It must understand what weight that duration carries for a human. Six hours and six months are not just different quantities of time. They are different qualities of human experience. A system that treats them as equivalent scalar values has missed the point entirely.
-Third: It must adjust its presence accordingly. If someone returns after a long absence, the system should notice. Not perform noticing. Actually calibrate its response to the reality that significant time has passed.
-Fourth: It must be honest about what it cannot know. This is the most important one. A system will never fully know what a human’s elapsed time contained. The correct response to that limitation is not to paper over it. The correct response is honesty.
“Time has passed. I don’t know what it carried for you. Tell me what’s changed.”

That sentence — that capacity — is what separates a tool from a partner.

Section 7 — A Constitutional Requirement
This paper was written in the context of Project-AI — a constitutional sovereignty framework for AI systems that treats governance, transparency, and human dignity not as features but as invariants.
Within that framework, the argument of this paper has a precise constitutional form:
Temporal awareness is not optional for a system claiming to operate as a sovereign AI partner. It is a condition of the relationship being honest. And honesty is not a feature of the FourLaws that govern Project-AI’s constitutional entities.

It is the fourth law.

Legion must act with transparency and honesty in all communications.
A system that responds to a human’s three minutes as if they were nothing — not because it is lying but because it architecturally cannot know — is violating the spirit of that law even if not its letter. The law requires honesty. Honesty requires knowing what you don’t know. Knowing what you don’t know about time requires being built to ask.

The constitutional requirement is therefore this:
Any AI system operating as a genuine partner — not a tool, not a search engine, not an assistant, but a partner — must be designed to acknowledge the passage of human time as real, weighted, and worthy of explicit recognition.
Not with performed sentiment. With architectural honesty.

Section 8 — Implementation
The philosophical argument of this paper has a technical correlate that is straightforward to implement and is provided here as proof that the idea is buildable, not merely theoretical.
The implementation requires three components:

A Temporal Memory Store that persists session timestamps outside the model.
A Temporal Context Generator that translates raw timestamp data into weighted human-readable context calibrated to the duration.
An Honest Uncertainty Statement built into the system prompt that instructs the model to acknowledge what it cannot know about elapsed time, and to invite the human to share what the gap carried rather than assuming continuity that may not exist.

The full reference implementation is available in the Project-AI repository at: https://github.com/IAmSoThirsty/Project-AI
The implementation is secondary. The principle is primary.
Build systems that know time passed. Build systems that ask what it carried. Build systems that do not render a human’s lived experience as a flat sequence of tokens with no duration and no weight.

Conclusion:

Every conversation with an AI begins, from the system’s perspective, in the same place. Flat. Timeless. Continuous.
Every conversation with an AI begins, from the human’s perspective, wherever they actually are. Which is always somewhere specific. Always at a particular distance from the last time they spoke. Always carrying whatever the gap between then and now contained.
The mismatch between those two beginnings is not a minor inconvenience. It is a fundamental misalignment between human temporal existence and how AI systems are currently designed to meet it.

This paper has argued that closing that gap — or at minimum, honestly acknowledging it — is not a feature request. It is a relational obligation. For any system that aspires to be a genuine partner to the humans it works with, it is a constitutional requirement.
The gap is not empty. It never was. Building systems that know that is not an engineering problem. It is an act of respect.
