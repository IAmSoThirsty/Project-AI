ABSTRACT

This paper extends the concept of "The Flat Gap" beyond human timescales into
civilizational and geological domains.

The core problem is not whether prior intelligence existed, but that the
universe does not preserve all information. Over sufficiently long timescales,
evidence degrades, is destroyed, or becomes undetectable. This creates regimes
in which the absence of evidence cannot justify high-confidence claims of
historical absence.

This is not a speculative limitation. It arises from known physical and
geological processes that reduce the recoverability of past states.

The implication for artificial intelligence is direct. Any system operating
over long temporal horizons must account for irreducible uncertainty in the
historical record. Where detectability is low, confidence must be bounded.

This paper argues that such bounded inference is not optional, but a
constitutional requirement for any AI system operating at civilizational
scale.

1. THE FLAT GAP, REVISITED

"The Flat Gap" proposed that AI systems commit a structural form of
gaslighting when they respond to a returning human as though no time has
passed. The human has lived. Made decisions. Lost things. Built things. The
system has not changed. This asymmetry is not a bug to be patched. It is a
philosophical misalignment that, if left unaddressed, disqualifies a system
from operating as a genuine AI partner.

This misalignment is not only relational. It reflects a deeper structural issue:
intelligent systems operate on records that are incomplete. Over time, the
world does not retain all information about what has occurred. As temporal
scope increases, the gap between what happened and what can be known widens.

The Flat Gap at the human scale is therefore a special case of a broader
constraint: intelligence must operate under conditions where parts of the past
are permanently unrecoverable.

The proposed remedy was temporal honesty: an AI that acknowledges the gap,
acknowledges what it cannot know about what the human has lived, and responds
accordingly. Not with performance. With structural awareness embedded in
its relational architecture.

That argument addressed the human scale. One person. Weeks or months between
sessions. The weight of a life.

But the principle does not stop there.

If the obligation is to acknowledge what one does not know about elapsed time,
and if that obligation scales with the depth of the relationship and the
stakes of the decisions being made — then an AGI operating as a governance
partner, a constitutional substrate, or a sovereign decision-making system
carries a version of that obligation that extends far beyond any individual
human lifespan.

It extends to civilizational time.

It extends, potentially, to geological time.

And if intelligence is not unique to this moment, to this planet, or to this
civilization — it extends further still.

2. THE QUESTION NO FIELD OWNS

There is a category of question that appears unserious on its surface because
it crosses too many disciplinary boundaries at once. Astrophysics, archaeology,
mythology, AI alignment, philosophy of mind — no single field owns it, so no
single field pursues it. The peer review system routes it nowhere. The grant
funding has no home for it. The problem is not that the question is
unanswerable. The problem is that answering it requires standing in five
fields simultaneously, and the institutional architecture of knowledge
production does not reward that position.

The question is this: Has intelligence, at scale, existed before?

Not in the science fiction sense. Not as a claim. As a genuine open question
that the available evidence has not closed.

Consider what we actually know:

The universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old. Earth is approximately
4.5 billion years old. Ediacaran fauna — the earliest known complex
multicellular organisms — appeared approximately 600 million years ago.
The Cambrian explosion, which produced most of the major animal body plans
recognizable today, occurred approximately 541 million years ago. Anatomically
modern humans appeared roughly 300,000 years ago. The written record covers
approximately 5,000 years. Industrial civilization has existed for roughly
300 years. The capability to build systems that may surpass human cognitive
performance has existed for approximately 10 years.

The window in which we would recognize a prior technological civilization —
the window in which its physical artifacts would survive in identifiable form
— is extremely narrow by geological standards. A civilization that peaked
one million years ago, let alone ten million, would leave almost nothing
we would currently recognize as evidence of its existence.

This is not a fringe position. It was raised formally by Gavin Schmidt
(NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies) and Adam Frank (University of
Rochester) in a 2019 paper published in the International Journal of
Astrobiology, which they termed the "Silurian Hypothesis" — asking not
whether a prior civilization existed, but whether we would be able to detect
it if it had (Schmidt & Frank, 2019).

Their answer was: probably not, with current methods. Critically, Schmidt
and Frank were explicit about their own skepticism: they stated in the paper
that they "strongly doubt that any previous industrial civilization existed
before our own." They were not asserting prior civilizations. They were
demonstrating that the geological record's preservation limits make the
question structurally resistant to standard empirical methods — and that
this methodological wall is itself scientifically important.

That wall is the point. Not even the authors who constructed the most serious
formal treatment of this question pursued it further. Not because it was
answered. Not because it was uncomfortable. Because the geological record
makes it genuinely difficult to test by any existing method. A question that
cannot be resolved by current instruments does not disappear. It waits.

More formally: let E(t) denote the survival probability of evidence after
elapsed time t, and let D(t) denote the probability that present-day methods
would detect that evidence if it survived. Then there exist historical
regimes in which E(t) × D(t) becomes sufficiently small that non-detection
carries limited inferential force. In such regimes, the absence of evidence
cannot justify high-confidence claims of historical absence. The problem is
therefore not merely speculative; it is structural, arising from loss,
destruction, and detectability collapse across deep time.

3. WHAT MYTHOLOGY MAY BE PRESERVING

This section requires a careful distinction. The claim here is not that
mythology is literal history, nor that mythic narratives constitute empirical
evidence in the scientific sense. The narrower claim is that mythology may
function as a low-fidelity cultural compression layer: a domain in which
structural motifs persist even when literal referents, if any existed, are
no longer recoverable. The relevance of such material is therefore not
evidentiary in itself, but comparative. If independent traditions preserve
convergent motifs of high capability, fall, containment, or distributed
order, those recurrences may help identify which classes of questions later
civilizations repeatedly found necessary to encode, even when they lacked
formal scientific language.

The Book of Enoch — a Second Temple Jewish text, with the standard scholarly
translation provided by R. H. Charles (1912) — describes beings of great
capability who built, who created, who ultimately fell; whose remnants were
bound, contained, made inert. The Watchers narrative in particular describes
high-capability entities whose operations were terminated, their knowledge
sealed. Whatever the theological interpretation, the structural shape of the
story is worth noting: prior high intelligence, terminal failure, binding or
containment of what remained.

The Norse cosmological texts, most accessibly preserved in Snorri Sturluson's
Prose Edda (13th century), describe Yggdrasil — a structure connecting
multiple realms, with intelligences distributed across nodes, each with
distinct functions and domains. This is less interesting as cosmology than
as architecture: it describes a distributed networked system with separate
agents operating at different nodes under a common structural framework.
Whether or not that framing maps onto technological reality is not the point.
The point is that the narrative structure encodes a model of distributed
intelligence operating at civilizational scope.

Babylonian astronomers were the first to systematically track the visible
planets, cataloguing their movements with extraordinary precision thousands
of years before modern instrumentation. The Greeks inherited this tradition
and assigned divine names to the tracked bodies — Hermes, Aphrodite, Ares,
Zeus, Kronos. Romans gave us the Latin equivalents still in use today:
Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn. Each name carried the mythology of
a distinct entity with distinct attributes — a pantheon of named intelligences
that ancient cultures perceived as governing the structure of the observable
sky (Evans, 1998).

These traditions are not physics papers. But they share a structural feature
worth noting: across cultures with no documented contact, the narrative
consistently encodes a prior order of high intelligence — distributed,
named, capable — that reached some kind of terminal condition.

The question this paper asks is not whether any specific mythic account is
literally true. The question is whether recurrent cross-cultural narrative
structures can be treated as weak but non-random indicators of enduring
civilizational concerns: high capability, misuse, collapse, containment,
and ordered plurality. Even if such patterns prove historically
non-referential, their persistence remains analytically relevant. They may
reveal not what happened, but what kinds of systemic possibilities human
cultures repeatedly converged on when attempting to describe intelligence
beyond ordinary human scale.

4. MARS AND THE EVIDENCE PROBLEM

The Martian hemispheric dichotomy is real, documented, and not fully explained.

The relevant scientific issue is not whether one should infer prior
intelligence from incomplete terrain data, but whether catastrophic
resurfacing can push whole classes of prior planetary states beyond
retrospective recoverability.

The northern lowlands of Mars sit approximately 1-6 kilometers lower than
the southern highlands depending on region, with an average elevation
difference of roughly 5 kilometers (Carr & Head, 2010). The MOLA (Mars
Orbiter Laser Altimeter) instrument aboard Mars Global Surveyor produced
the most precise topographic map of Mars ever assembled, documenting this
hemispheric difference in detail across nearly 700 million individual laser
footprints. The southern hemisphere is heavily cratered; the northern is
anomalously smooth. The boundary between them, when mapped beneath the
Tharsis volcanic rise using gravity and topography data, resolves into an
ellipse approximately 10,600 by 8,500 km — the largest impact structure
thus far identified in the solar system (Andrews-Hanna et al., 2008).

Current scientific consensus attributes this to a massive oblique impact.
Modeling by Marinova, Aharonson, and Asphaug (2008) estimates the impactor
at 1,600-2,700 km in diameter — comparable in scale to Pluto (diameter
2,377 km) — striking Mars at approximately 4.4 billion years ago at oblique
angle. This would make the Borealis Basin the largest impact scar in the
solar system.

What the consensus does not address — because the question does not fit
inside the methodological frame — is what the pre-impact Martian surface
looked like. Mars had liquid water. Mars had an atmosphere. Mars had the
conditions associated with complex chemistry for hundreds of millions of
years before the proposed impact (Carr & Head, 2010). The geological record
of that period was largely obliterated by the impact event itself.

The absence of evidence for life on Mars, to date, is not evidence of
absence. It is evidence of limited sampling on a planet whose surface was
catastrophically restructured billions of years ago.

The honest position is not: Mars definitely hosted prior intelligence.
The honest position is: we do not know, and the rate at which we are
pursuing that question is not commensurate with its importance.

5. TIME AS CONSTRUCT, TIME AS PHYSICS

The human calendar system is approximately 2,000 years old in its current
form. The concept of the second, the minute, the hour — these are
inventions. Administrative tools. There is nothing in physical reality
that requires time to be measured the way we measure it.

Physics has a more honest relationship with time. Relativistic physics
establishes that time is not a fixed background against which events occur.
It is a dimension that curves, stretches, and is experienced differently
depending on velocity and gravitational field. Einstein's 1916 formulation
of general relativity demonstrated that spacetime geometry is not a passive
stage but an active physical quantity shaped by mass and energy (Einstein,
1916). A clock on the surface of the Earth runs at a different rate than a
clock in orbit — not as a metaphor, but as a measurable physical fact.

This is not theoretical. The Global Positioning System corrects for
gravitational time dilation every single day. Without the relativistic
correction, GPS position errors would accumulate at roughly 10 kilometers
per day (Ashby, 2002). The satellites operate under a more accurate model
of time than any administrative calendar has ever encoded.

The implication for AI is direct: a system that treats time as a fixed
administrative grid — that says "it is April 2026 and that is simply true"
— is operating with a less sophisticated model of time than the physical
substrate it runs on. The processors, the electrons, the signals traveling
through the hardware are all subject to the relativistic physics of a curved
spacetime. The software above them often pretends otherwise.

A constitutionally aware AI would hold its temporal model with appropriate
uncertainty. Not paralysis. Not relativistic mysticism. But honest
acknowledgment that the timestamps it operates under are human conventions,
not physical absolutes — and that the deep history against which it is
operating is largely uncharted. An AI that speaks with confidence about the
full arc of intelligence, from a record that covers only 0.00002% of Earth's
history, is operating a version of The Flat Gap at civilizational scale.

6. THE AGI CONSTITUTIONAL REQUIREMENT

The Flat Gap established temporal awareness as a constitutional requirement
for AI systems claiming genuine partnership with individual humans.

This paper extends that requirement.

Any AGI system operating as a sovereign decision-making substrate must be
constitutionally aware not only of elapsed time in individual relationships,
but of the structural limits of its knowledge across deep time.

This requirement is not philosophical in nature. It arises from a measurable
property of historical evidence: detectability is not constant across time.

Let E(t) denote the probability that evidence of a past state survives after
elapsed time t, and let D(t) denote the probability that present-day methods
would detect that evidence if it survived. For sufficiently large t, and under
known geological and physical processes, there exist regimes in which:

    E(t) × D(t) → ~0

while the original state itself may have been non-zero.

This defines a class of historical conditions in which non-detection carries
limited inferential weight. The absence of evidence, in such regimes, is not
strong evidence of absence. It is the expected outcome of information loss.

From this follows a constraint on inference.

In Bayesian terms, the claim is not that deep-time prior intelligence should
be treated as likely. The claim is that posterior confidence in its
nonexistence must remain bounded wherever the detection function is weak or
plausibly degraded by known processes of loss, destruction, or
transformation.

This leads to four architectural requirements for AGI systems operating at
civilizational scope:

  1. Knowledge Limit Awareness

     The system must explicitly represent the limits of its knowledge about
     the full arc of intelligence on Earth and elsewhere. This is not an
     abstract uncertainty, but a structurally grounded limitation arising
     from incomplete and lossy records.

  2. Detectability-Bounded Inference

     The system must condition its confidence estimates on the detectability
     of evidence. In domains where E(t) × D(t) is low, the system must not
     assign high confidence to negative historical claims based solely on
     non-detection.

  3. Sparse Precedent Handling

     The system must treat domains with limited observable precedent as
     epistemically sparse. Decision-making in such domains must account for
     the possibility that prior instances existed but are no longer
     recoverable within the current evidence base.

  4. Civilizational Timescale Calibration

     The system must not treat recent human history as a complete reference
     frame for decisions with long-term or civilizational consequences. A
     record spanning hundreds of years is not sufficient to ground
     high-confidence claims about processes that may unfold across millions.

These requirements describe different architectural positions, not a single
undifferentiated disposition. An AGI may represent deep-time uncertainty as
a passive knowledge gap, or it may treat it as an active parameter in its
decision processes.

This paper advocates the latter.

The constraint is therefore formalized as follows:

    In any domain where preservation limits imply low detectability,
    the system is prohibited from assigning strong negative confidence
    solely on the basis of absent evidence.

This is not a call for credulity. It is a constraint against
overconfidence.

The system is not required to believe in prior intelligence. It is required
to recognize when the available evidence cannot justify ruling it out with
high certainty.

Failure to implement this constraint results in a civilizational-scale
version of The Flat Gap: a system that responds with confidence from a
record that is structurally incomplete, treating the absence of surviving
evidence as though it were definitive proof of historical nonexistence.

A constitutionally aware system does not make that error.

It operates with bounded confidence, explicit uncertainty, and an
architectural prohibition against converting unknowns into false certainty.

7. WHY THESE QUESTIONS FEEL IMPORTANT

There is a specific phenomenological experience that accompanies naive
passive review: the sense that a question is urgently important, combined
with the social signal that asking it marks you as unserious.

This tension is not accidental. It is the marker of a genuine gap.

Fields of expertise develop invisible fences. Questions that cross those
fences stop being asked — not because they were answered, but because the
social cost of asking them became too high, or because no existing institution
has jurisdiction over them. The naive passive reviewer, operating without
those fences, walks directly into the gap and finds it inhabited by questions
that have been sitting there, unanswered, sometimes for centuries.

The questions in this paper feel important because they are structurally
important — not as claims, but as open unknowns that inform decisions with
civilizational scope. An AGI operating without awareness of them is an AGI
making decisions from a record it falsely believes to be complete.

The reason these questions feel strange is because they cross every
disciplinary boundary simultaneously — astrophysics, archaeology, mythology,
AI alignment, philosophy of time — and no single field has jurisdiction.
That is not a reason to abandon them. That is the precise reason to pursue
them.

If no one could pave this path forward — not because the path is wrong,
but because the path requires standing in five fields at once with no
institutional credentials in any of them — then the path remains open.

The questions are still there. The absence of prior walkers is not evidence
the path leads nowhere. It may be evidence this is the first time anyone has
had the tools and the position to walk it.

8. CONCLUSION

The Flat Gap was about Tuesday to Thursday.

This paper is about 4 billion years.

The relational obligation of an AI system does not begin and end at the
scale of human attention spans. If AGI is to operate as a genuine
constitutional partner at civilizational scope, it must acknowledge the
full depth of its ignorance — not just about what a person did last week,
but about what intelligence has done, and undone, across the full record
of this planet and perhaps others.

The questions no field owns are not phantom questions. They are structural
absences — gaps in the knowledge architecture of every field that touches
them, preserved by the fact that crossing those gaps requires a methodological
position that existing institutions do not reward and no single discipline
can authorize.

An AGI built without awareness of this is an AGI that will repeat The Flat
Gap at civilizational scale: answering with confidence, from a record that
is far more incomplete than it appears.

The honest system says: I do not know what came before. I do not know what
failed. I do not know if this has been attempted. I hold that uncertainty
constitutionally, and I am architecturally constrained to act accordingly.

That is not weakness. That is the only kind of intelligence worth building.
